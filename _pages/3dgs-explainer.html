Section 1.5

The 2D Gaussian (Quadratic Form)

Let's extend the Gaussian into 2D by combining two independent variables. The key idea is that products of Gaussians turn into sums inside the exponential $\exp{(\cdot)}$.

From 1D to 2D: take $\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ and the 2D Gaussian becomes:
$$ g(\boldsymbol{x}) = \exp \left( -\frac12 (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) \right) $$
How? - (derivation)
$$ \begin{align*} g(\boldsymbol{x}) &= g(x_1,x_2) = g(x_1)g(x_2) \qquad \text{joint probability density} \\ \end{align*} $$ $$ g_1(x_1) = \exp \left(-\frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma_1^2}\right), \qquad g_2(x_2) = \exp \left(-\frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}\right)\\ $$
$$ \begin{align*} g(x_1,x_2) &= \exp \left(-\frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma_1^2}\right) \exp \left(-\frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}\right) \\ &= \exp \left( -\frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma_1^2} -\frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2} \right) & \exp(a)\exp(b)=\exp(a+b) \\ &= \exp \left( -\frac12 \left[ \frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} + \frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2} \right] \right) \end{align*} $$
Now define the deviation vector ${\boldsymbol{\delta}}$ and the precision matrix $\boldsymbol{D}$:
$$ \boldsymbol{\delta} = \boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1-\mu_1 \\ x_2-\mu_2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{D} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sigma_2^2} \end{bmatrix} $$
$$ \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{\delta}^\top {\boldsymbol{D}} \boldsymbol{\delta} &= (x_1-\mu_1)\frac{1}{\sigma_1^2}(x_1-\mu_1) + (x_2-\mu_2)\frac{1}{\sigma_2^2}(x_2-\mu_2) \\ &= \frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} + \frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2} \end{align*} $$
Substituting back into the Gaussian expression:
$$ g(\boldsymbol{x}) = \exp \left( -\frac12 (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{D} (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) \right) $$
Connecting $\boldsymbol{D}$ to the covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$:
$$ \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_2^2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = \boldsymbol{D} $$
$$ g(\boldsymbol{x}) = \exp \left( -\frac12 (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) \right) $$
Section 1.75

From Probability Density to Geometric Footprint

Up to this point, we have treated the Gaussian as a probability density. In graphics, we reinterpret the same function as a geometric footprint.

Nothing about the equation changes. What changes is how we use its parameters.

$$ g(x) = \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2}(x-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu) \right), \qquad x \in \mathbb{R}^2 $$

In this setting:

• the mean $\mu$; controls where the footprint is centered
• the covariance $\Sigma$; controls its shape and orientation

Rather than measuring uncertainty, $\Sigma$ now describes how far the footprint extends in each direction.

Mean as position

The mean $\mu$ $\in$ $\mathbb{R}^2$ specifies the center of the Gaussian.

Changing $\mu$ translates the entire density field without altering its shape:

$$ x \;\mapsto\; x - \mu $$

In rendering terms, this is exactly what we expect from a point primitive: moving the position moves the footprint.

Covariance as shape

A Gaussian goes on forever, but for rendering we need a finite geometric primitive that is easier to work with. We can get this by clipping the Gaussian with a constant $c$, such that $g(\boldsymbol{x}) = c$.

Enable gaussian clipping for a level set

The covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times2}$ determines the shape and orientation of the Gaussian footprint in the plane. Fixing a constant $c \in (0,1)$, the level set is equivalent to $(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) = c$. In two dimensions, these level sets are ellipses.

Recall that $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is symmetric and positive semi-definite, guaranteeing that all eigenvalues are non-negative and that the level sets are closed curves.

$$ \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & \sigma_{12} \\ \sigma_{12} & \sigma_2^2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Linear algebra gives a factorization that is useful for controlling the Gaussian's shape:

$$ \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\boldsymbol{Q}^\top, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\lambda_2), \qquad \lambda_1,\lambda_2 \ge 0 $$

Here $\boldsymbol{Q}$ is a rotation matrix whose columns are the ellipse’s principal directions, and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is a diagonal matrix whose entries $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ control the spread of the Gaussian along those axes.

$$ \boldsymbol{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{Q}^\top = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} $$

Taking this factorization and substituting into the fixed level set $g(x)=c$ yields:

$$ g(\boldsymbol{x}) = (x-\mu)^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (x-\mu) = (\boldsymbol{Q}^\top(x-\mu))^\top \boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{-1} (\boldsymbol{Q}^\top(x-\mu)) = c $$

Let $u=\boldsymbol{Q}^\top(x-\mu)$, then:

$$ \frac{u_1^2}{\lambda_1} + \frac{u_2^2}{\lambda_2} = c $$

That is exactly the equation of an ellipse. So:

• larger $\lambda_i$ → larger radius along axis $i$
• smaller $\lambda_i$ → smaller radius (faster decay along that axis)

This is how eigenvalues control scale.

For rendering, it's better to use a slightly different form of this eigendecomposition that protects against sign ambiguity and degenerate values.

Introduce $\boldsymbol{R}$ as a rotation matrix (same role as $\boldsymbol{Q}$), then define $\boldsymbol{S} = \mathrm{diag}(s_1, s_2)$ where $s_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

$$ \boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{S}^\top \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \boldsymbol{R}\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{S}^\top \boldsymbol{R}^\top $$
This parameterization guarantees that $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ remains symmetric and positive for all slider values.

Changing $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ reshapes the footprint. This is the geometric object we will render -- and eventually project, rasterize, and optimize.

Section 2.5

From Ellipses to Ellipsoids

What happens when we add one more dimension?

So far, we have described a Gaussian footprint in 2 dimensions, where the input $\boldsymbol{x}$ lives in the plane and the level sets form ellipses. When the input is in 3 dimensions i.e., $\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, the mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ becomes a point in space, and the covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ becomes a $3\times3$ matrix.

This dimensional change means that geometry of the level sets no longer traces curves -- it traces closed surfaces. An ellipse becomes an ellipsoid. We still have $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \boldsymbol{R}\,\boldsymbol{S}\,\boldsymbol{S}^\top\,\boldsymbol{R}^\top$, except $\boldsymbol{R}$ is a 3D rotation and $\boldsymbol{S} = \mathrm{diag}(s_1, s_2, s_3)$ controls scale along three axes.
